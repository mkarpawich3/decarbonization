Introduction

In 1988, James Hansen testified before Congress that oil, coal, and other carbon-producing fossil fuels were responsible for global warming and would have a devastating impact on the world.

In 1995, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change issued its Second Assessment of climate change, recognizing the conclusions reached by Hansen and others on an international stage.

In response, the oil and natural gas industries of Oklahoma established the Oklahoma Energy Resources Board in 1993, setting out on their restoration and education initiatives that still exist today.

The restoration initiative restores abandoned oil sites, shifting the temporality of the charges levied against them by the IPCC and others to the past.

The education initiative creates educational programs for Oklahoma schools, garnering future in-state support for the oil and natural gas industries to ensure their prolonged survival.

Among these programs, the Petro Pete series is arguably the most shocking.

In one book — Boomer Burrow — the OERB presents a fable in which the rumblings of restoration equipment disturb a community of cartoon animals until a hardhat-wearing dog-turned-liaison reassures them by explaining the benefits of this restorative process on the environment.

The shock comes from the fact that this process is not nearly as effective as purported by the OERB.

While the OERB has worked to “restore” oil sites and decontaminate their surrounding environment...

the state of Oklahoma has experienced a record-breaking number of earthquakes.

The OERB might be able to restore these sites on the surface, but they — among all of the other abandoned sites in the region — create an internal scar on and under the surface of the Earth that renders Oklahoma communities emotionally and physically shaken.

In response to Boomer Burrow, we present you with a sequel:

The Real Rumblings
Following the same set of characters as Boomer Burrow, this narrative paints a darker image of the OERB and the effect it has on the environment.

The Real Rumblings



A story by John An, Ethan Das, and Max Karpawich.

“All the animals of Bummer Burrow, to the tree trunk at once!” Exclaimed Redmond Rabbit.

It was time for the spring seasonal meeting once again in Bummer Burrow. Only this time, things hadn't been so good.

All the groggy animals gather together, discussing their hibernation. Shiloh Skunk turns and says to Bedelia Bobcat:

“This rumbling we have been getting has really ruined my winter. My children and I have been getting no sleep all season!”

“Quiet everyone!” Redmond Rabbit yelled. “I’ve brought us here to discuss the rumblings I’m sure all of you have also felt all winter.”

“It’s been getting worse! I can’t sleep anymore!” cried Boris Bobcat.

“Does anyone know where it comes from?” Redmond Rabbit asked.


No one responded.

“We can go ask RePete about the humans, just like last time,” said Sinclair Scissortail.

“Good idea, Sinclair,” responded Redmond Rabbit.

“RePete, do you have any idea what is going on with the rumblings?” said Redmond Rabbit.

“Well, I have no idea Redmond Rabbit! All I know is that it can’t be the humans, because they have all finished cleaning up Bummer Burrow, and moved on to the next town. I think all of you can stop and wait for it to pass!” exclaimed RePete, cool as a cucumber.

Everyone agreed.

That was a mighty good plan!

But the rumblings kept going for weeks and weeks, and Bummer Burrow started falling apart.

It was too much for the little community to hold up against, and the rumblings just kept getting stronger and stronger!

One week, when all of Bummer Burrow woke up, they realized there was no rumbling to disturb their sleep. The days went by and still nothing happened.

“RePete was right!” said Sinclair, “The rumblings really did stop!”

All of Bummer Burrow held a festival, including RePete, to commemorate the end of the rumblings and a return to normal life. All was happy again!

Or so the animals of Bummer Burrow thought...

At the end of the week, something terrible happened! During the night, a powerful rumbling started, stronger than all the rest.

The animals sprung from their beds and scrambled about, unable to find their way in the dark.

All of Bummer Burrow was awake and had escaped above ground. When the dust settled, everyone could see that their Bummer Burrow was no more!

All of their homes had caved in, and they had nowhere to go.

“What will we ever do!” cried Boris Bobcat.

“It seems like this mess is something we cannot fix,” said Redmond Rabbit, “We have no choice but to find a new burrow to make our home.”

“Maybe the humans were wrong. Maybe they didn’t really fix Bummer Burrow,” sobbed Shadow Skunk.

“We have no time to lose everyone. Let’s go!” exclaimed Redmond Rabbit.

The animals of Boomer Burrow took one last look at what was left.

Then, together, they went way from Boomer Burrow, in search of a new home.

The End.

Grove Park Poster

The message of the poster is for Grove Park residents to “go green” through gradual adoption of electric transportation. The visual journey of the audience starts in the top-left corner with the command: “GO”. Following the arrows around the length of the edge, this message grows into a statement: “Go Green Grove Park”.

More than just directional suggestions, these arrows also replicate the recycle symbol, adhering to the theme of sustainability. As the viewer returns to the “GO”, they are then directed towards the car because of how it visually imbalances the piece.

At this point, the viewer knows that the piece commands them to “go green”, but is still unsure as to how.

The car directly provides context to their question, while also introducing two new ideas.

On the one hand, the upward angle of the car — coupled with the graph-like incline through the middle of the piece — suggests growth over time. On the other hand, the placement of the car at the base of this incline suggests that the viewer still has work left to reach the top.

Finished with the car, the viewer continues along the length of the incline to reach the center of the piece.

Knowing that they must “go green” in context to transportation, the viewer ideally understands the juxtaposition of the plug-plant with the gas-handle-root imagery as a symbol of the juxtaposition between electric and gas-fueled vehicles.

Several elements of the piece work together to create this juxtaposition.

Coupled with the direct comparison of smooth edges along the top image and jagged ones along the bottom, the respective color and position of each region (green and red, top and bottom) imply a good and bad relationship through themes of Heaven and Hell.

Furthermore, the green color aligns the electricity theme that is suggested by the plug imagery with the decarbonization movement through its agreement with the plant contours of the top image.

Meanwhile, the red color reinforces the gas theme suggested by the gas handle imagery as the standard color for American gasoline cans.

Together, all of these design elements conclude the message of the piece by providing the means by which the viewer can perform the “go green” action through transportation:

BY GOING ELECTRIC.

Other elements also develop a hopeful tone for the piece.

As referenced earlier, the words “GROVE” and “PARK” are composed of a majority of red letters. Paired with the lowered position of the car along the incline, this majority of red letters expresses a present negative condition of the Grove Park community with respect to electric transportation.

However, the placement and upward angle of the car in the green region and the open space above it both imply that the Grove Park community has an opportunity — room — for growth over time.

Thus, this poster not only commands the audience to “go green” through electric transportation...

IT ENCOURAGES IT.
